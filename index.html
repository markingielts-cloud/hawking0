Why this helps you write better stories
Great writers don't choose tenses randomly — every tense is a deliberate signal to the reader about when something happened and why it matters. This exercise trains you to read like a writer: to notice the choices an author makes and understand the logic behind them. When you can explain why a tense is used, you can start making those same choices intentionally in your own writing — giving your stories a clearer timeline and a stronger sense of control.

Why this helps you write better stories
Reading a story is one thing — reconstructing it from the inside is another. By filling in every verb yourself, you are forced to think about the flow of time in the narrative: what happened first, what was already finished, what was still in progress, and what lay ahead. This is exactly the thinking a writer does before putting a sentence on the page. The more fluent this becomes, the more naturally your own writing will handle complex timelines without getting tangled.

Why this helps you write better stories
Experienced writers think in tools, not just words. They ask: do I need to move the timeline here, or surprise the reader, or go inside a character's head? Before they can answer that question, they need a mental toolkit — a set of expressions organised by what job they do. This exercise builds that toolkit. Once you know which phrases control time, which create suspense, which report speech, and which reveal inner thoughts, you can reach for exactly the right one when you need it — rather than repeating the same phrases over and over.

Why this helps you write better stories
Every sentence in a story creates an expectation in the reader's mind — a particular kind of continuation that feels logically right. This exercise trains your ear to hear that logic. Phrases like "Up until that point" or "It dawned on me" don't just add colour — they make a grammatical and narrative promise about what must come next. When you internalise these patterns, your sentences will flow more naturally and your reader will never feel lost or confused by a sudden shift in direction.
